Background

For my user-centered design course, I worked in a team of 4 in order to create a user-centered product for urban beekeepers. After conducting research with beekeepers, our team found that many beekeepers struggle with dedicating time to their multiple beehives efficiently, especially in the winter. They were also worried about noticing and responding to threats like diseases to their hives. With these beekeeping pain points in mind, our team designed a mobile, intuitive monitoring system. Our product focuses on helping beekeepers:

  • Monitor their bees remotely
  • Maintain beehive health factors
  • Record important beehive statistics
  • Continue beekeeping with inexpensive costs

Our team went through the full user-centered design process to create Hive Queen. Please view a shortened version of our work and design process below. For the full write-up, please click this link here.

Design Process

User Research / Four Weeks

For this project, our team chose a user group that none of us were familiar with in order to eliminate existing bias. We chose beekeepers randomly and conducted in-depth interviews with four different beekeepers. Using this information, we created two user personas based on the two important types of beekeepers we identified to evaluate the different pain points that beekeepers currently have. We then used those personas to create a user journey map to further empathize with the different emotions that beekeepers feel when interacting with their hive. After four weeks of research, our team started to think of solutions through brainstorming and focusing on our decided design requirements.

Design Requirements / One Week

Using the information from our research, we came up with a list of design requirements that we wanted to address with our solution. Using these design requirements, each team member created two storyboards with different solutions for our design requirements. We discussed these solutions as a team and decided which one we would use. Our chosen solution was a physical hive monitoring system connected to a mobile application for beekeepers to use. This combination would allow beekeepers to easily overcome their pain points of not being able to check on their beehives due to location or weather.

After choosing a solution, our team focused on the information architecture of the application and hive monitoring system before designing prototypes. We wanted to make sure users can easily access information and that the hierarchy of the information made sense. Using this information architecture map, our team created paper prototypes and Figma to draw our mobile application and hive system.

Prototyping / One Week

For the next week, we focused on creating prototypes and wireframes. We focused on creating low fidelity prototypes, using pencil and paper for both the mobile application and hive monitoring system. These prototypes were meant for us to conduct usability testing with.

Usability Testing / One Week

Then after creating our prototypes, we had to conduct user testing in order to make sure our product was actually working how we intended it to for the users. Although we could not schedule testing with beekeepers themselves, we managed to test with a variety of college students. Our team had testers conduct three different tasks while thinking out loud that each had a variety of steps to complete. We took notes and we found out through first impressions and task walk-throughs that our mobile application could be made more efficient and simpler. Our team realized we were trying to add too many features that took away from the purpose of the hive system. Thanks to the user testing, we revised our designs and then created high fidelity mock-ups.

High Fidelity Mock-Ups / Two Weeks

Our high fidelity mock-ups for the mobile application were made in Figma, with 22 different screens, and the physical hive monitoring system was designed in Solidworks and 3D printed. These mock-ups took a week to design. The last part of the project was presenting it to various companies in order to see if it was something that they would sponsor. We created a presentation detailing our design process and ended with a 100% rate of support for our project.

Reflection

Looking back on this project, it was definitely difficult to thoroughly create a product in just nine weeks. However, it was a great learning experience for our team. We learned a lot about the user centered design process as well as the beekeeping process. It was great to bond together as complete strangers and collaborate as a team over a long-term project.

Throughout the process, there were some moments where I felt that we were a little rushed and were not able to do exactly what we wanted. For the usability testing, I wish we would have had time to schedule user testing with beekeepers themselves to see how effective and feasible our product would be for them. I also would have liked more time to create higher fidelity mockups. But, these issues were great learning experiences as well that taught me the time constraints of research and testing in industry that I will have to face.
